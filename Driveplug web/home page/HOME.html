E-CAR TERMINOLOGY

The term "electric car" typically refers specifically to battery electric vehicles (BEVs) or all-electric cars, a type of electric vehicle (EV) that has an onboard rechargeable battery pack that can be plugged in and charged from the electric grid, and the electricity stored on the vehicle is the only energy source that provides propulsion for the wheels. The term generally refers to highway-capable automobiles, but there are also low-speed electric vehicles with limitations in terms of weight, power, and maximum speed that are allowed to travel on certain public roads. The latter are classified as Neighborhood Electric Vehicles (NEVs) in the United States, and as electric motorized quadricycles in Europe.

Electric cars can be powered by electricity from a variety of sources, including hydropower, solar power, nuclear power, and coal-fired power stations. The electricity may be stored onboard the car using a battery, flywheel, or supercapacitors. Vehicles making use of engines working on the principle of combustion can usually only derive their energy from a single or a few sources, usually non-renewable fossil fuels. A key advantage of electric vehicles is their ability to recover energy normally lost during braking as electricity to be restored to the on-board battery or sent back to the grid. Regenerative braking is used to slow the vehicle and can provide a significant efficiency improvement over vehicles that do not recapture energy during braking.

HOW E-CARS CHARGE

Electric cars can be charged from a variety of sources, including wall sockets, charging stations, and dedicated charging units. The speed of charging depends on the charging station's power output and the vehicle's onboard charger. The three main types of charging are slow, fast, and ultra-fast charging.

  • Slow charging: 3-4 hours
  • Fast charging: 20-30 minutes
  • Ultra-fast charging: 5-10 minutes

ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT OF E-CARS

Electric cars produce zero tailpipe emissions, which helps to reduce air pollution and combat climate change. They are also quieter than vehicles powered by internal combustion engines, which can reduce noise pollution. However, the environmental impact of electric cars depends on how the electricity used to charge them is generated. If the electricity comes from renewable sources like wind, solar, or hydropower, electric cars can be nearly emissions-free over their entire lifecycle.

The production of electric cars, however, can have a higher environmental impact than traditional cars due to the mining and processing of raw materials like lithium, cobalt, and nickel for batteries. To minimize the environmental impact of electric cars, it is important to recycle and reuse batteries and source materials responsibly.
